##### Final Business Recommendation

###### Executive Summary

A regression analysis was conducted to identify the business factors most strongly associated with monthly sales across retail stores. Three regression models were evaluated, including two simple linear regression models and one multiple regression model. The multiple regression model provided the best overall performance, explaining approximately **78.53%** of the variation in monthly sales (**R² = 0.7853**) and was statistically significant (**F-test p < 0.001**).

The analysis indicates that **marketing spend** and **customer footfall** are the strongest positive drivers of monthly sales, while **average discount percentage** shows a statistically significant negative relationship with sales after controlling for the other variables. The regional dummy variable included in the model was not statistically significant.

\---

###### Factors Most Strongly Associated with Monthly Sales

The regression results identified the following variables as having the strongest association with monthly sales:

###### 1\. Footfall

* Positive coefficient (approximately **33.52**)
* Highly statistically significant (**p < 0.001**)
* Strongest predictor in the multiple regression model.

**Business Interpretation**

Stores that attract more customers consistently achieve higher monthly sales. Increasing customer traffic is therefore one of the most effective ways to improve revenue.

\---

###### 2\. Marketing Spend

* Positive coefficient (approximately **2.13** in the simple regression model)
* Highly statistically significant (**p < 0.001**)

**Business Interpretation**

Higher marketing investment is associated with higher monthly sales. Marketing activities appear to generate measurable business value and should remain an important component of the company's growth strategy.

\---

###### 3\. Average Discount Percentage

* Negative coefficient
* Statistically significant (**p < 0.05**)

**Business Interpretation**

After controlling for other factors, higher discount levels are associated with lower monthly sales. This suggests that excessive discounting does not necessarily increase revenue and may reduce profitability or reflect weaker-performing stores.

\---

###### Variables Leadership Should Focus On

Based on the regression evidence, leadership should prioritize the following variables:

###### Increase Customer Footfall

* Invest in local advertising.
* Improve customer experience.
* Organize promotional events.
* Strengthen customer loyalty programs.

Since footfall is the strongest predictor of sales, initiatives that increase store visits are likely to produce the greatest business impact.

\---

###### Optimize Marketing Investment

Marketing expenditure has a consistently positive and statistically significant relationship with monthly sales.

Leadership should:

* Continue investing in marketing campaigns.
* Measure campaign return on investment (ROI).
* Allocate larger budgets to stores demonstrating the highest returns.

\---

###### Review Discount Strategy

Rather than relying on broad discounts, leadership should:

* Use targeted promotions.
* Personalize discounts for selected customer segments.
* Monitor the effectiveness of promotional campaigns.

This approach can improve sales while protecting profit margins.

\---

###### Variables That Should Not Be Over-Interpreted

###### Region Dummy Variable

The regional dummy variable included in the final model was **not statistically significant (p > 0.05)**.

This suggests that, after accounting for marketing spend, footfall, and discounts, regional differences alone do not explain variation in monthly sales.

Leadership should therefore avoid making strategic decisions based solely on geographic location without considering additional market factors.

\---

###### Recommended Business Actions

Based on the regression findings, the following actions are recommended:

1. Increase investment in marketing activities that have demonstrated positive returns.
2. Prioritize initiatives that increase customer footfall, including advertising, loyalty programs, and in-store events.
3. Use discounting strategically instead of implementing broad price reductions.
4. Monitor store performance using predictive analytics and investigate stores with unusually large residuals.
5. Continue collecting additional operational and customer data to improve future predictive models.

\---

###### Risks and Limitations

Although the regression model explains a substantial proportion of the variation in monthly sales, several limitations should be considered:

* The model does not include all possible business factors, such as customer demographics, weather, local economic conditions, product assortment, or competitor promotions.
* Regression assumes linear relationships between variables.
* Some predictors may be correlated with each other, which can affect coefficient estimates.
* Prediction errors remain for certain stores, as shown by the residual analysis.
* The model reflects historical data and may not fully predict future business conditions.

\---

##### Why Regression Shows Association Rather Than Causation

Regression analysis identifies **statistical associations** between variables but does **not prove cause-and-effect relationships**.

For example, the analysis shows that stores with higher marketing spend tend to have higher monthly sales. However, this does not necessarily mean that increasing marketing spend alone will always cause sales to increase. Other factors, such as store size, management quality, local demand, seasonality, or customer demographics, may influence both marketing investment and sales.

Similarly, the negative relationship between average discount percentage and monthly sales should not be interpreted as evidence that discounts directly reduce sales. It may instead reflect that stores experiencing weaker sales are more likely to offer larger discounts.

To establish causation, additional methods such as randomized controlled experiments, A/B testing, or longitudinal studies would be required.

\---

###### Final Recommendation

The multiple regression model provides strong evidence that **customer footfall** and **marketing spend** are the most important business drivers associated with monthly sales. Leadership should focus on increasing store traffic, optimizing marketing investments, and applying discount strategies selectively rather than broadly.

The model explains approximately **78.5%** of the variation in monthly sales, making it a valuable decision-support tool. However, business decisions should not rely solely on regression outputs. Management should combine these statistical insights with operational knowledge, market research, and controlled experiments to develop effective, evidence-based business strategies.
